# Executive Summary — Compliance Requirements for Raw Materials (Food & Dietary Supplements) in EU vs USA

## 1) Core Regulatory Philosophy

- **EU:** Preventive, pre-market–oriented system. Only authorized or assessed substances can be used; strong reliance on centralized legislation and scientific risk assessment.  
- **USA:** Post-market–oriented system. Manufacturers have broader flexibility but must ensure safety; FDA enforces compliance after market entry.

---

## 2) European Union (EU) — Raw Material Compliance

### A. Legal Classification
- Food supplements are treated as foods under EU law.  
- Key frameworks:
  - Regulation (EC) No 178/2002 (General Food Law)  
  - Directive 2002/46/EC (Food Supplements)

### B. Raw Material Authorization
- Positive lists apply:
  - Only approved vitamins and minerals and their permitted sources may be used.  
- Novel ingredients:
  - Require authorization under Novel Food Regulation if not used before 1997.  
- Botanicals and other substances may be subject to national or EU-specific rules.

### C. Safety and Risk Assessment
- Scientific evaluation required (often by EFSA):
  - Toxicological safety  
  - Bioavailability  
- EU may prohibit or restrict substances under Regulation 1925/2006.

### D. Purity and Composition
- Must meet:
  - Food-grade specifications  
  - Limits for contaminants (e.g., heavy metals)  
- Additives regulated under Regulation 1333/2008.

### E. Operator Responsibility
- Food business operator must ensure safety and compliance before market placement.

### F. Traceability and Controls
- Full traceability required  
- Mandatory ability to withdraw unsafe products  
- Some Member States require product notification before sale

---

## 3) United States (USA) — Raw Material Compliance

### A. Legal Framework
- Federal Food, Drug, and Cosmetic Act  
- Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act (DSHEA)

### B. Raw Material Eligibility
- Must qualify as dietary ingredients:
  - Vitamins, minerals, herbs, amino acids, etc.  
- New Dietary Ingredients (NDI):
  - Require FDA notification unless marketed before 1994

### C. Pre-Approval
- No general pre-market approval system for most ingredients  
- Manufacturer determines safety

### D. cGMP Requirements (21 CFR Part 111)
Key controls:
- Identity testing of each dietary ingredient  
- Supplier qualification  
- Quarantine until approval  
- Sampling and testing (chemical, microbiological)  
- Specifications for purity, strength, composition  
- Contamination and mix-up prevention

### E. Quality System
- Establish specifications for each component  
- Verify compliance with specifications  
- Maintain batch records and traceability  
- Laboratory testing required

### F. Post-Market Enforcement
- FDA enforces through:
  - Inspections  
  - Warning letters  
  - Recalls

---

## 4) Direct Comparison

| Area | EU | USA |
|------|----|-----|
| Regulatory approach | Pre-market authorization | Post-market enforcement |
| Ingredient eligibility | Positive lists; novel foods require approval | Broad categories; NDI notification |
| Safety responsibility | Shared with regulatory system | Manufacturer responsibility |
| Raw material testing | Required, less prescriptive | Highly prescriptive (cGMP) |
| Substance restrictions | Centralized lists | Case-by-case enforcement |
| Traceability | Mandatory | Required via GMP |
| Market entry | Often requires notification | No pre-approval (except NDI) |

---

## 5) Practical Implications

### EU
- Focus on legal eligibility of ingredients  
- Risk: non-authorized or novel substances  
- Strong regulatory gatekeeping

### USA
- Focus on manufacturing and quality control  
- Risk: contamination or misidentification  
- Greater flexibility with higher internal compliance burden

---

## 6) Bottom-Line

- **EU:** Only approved raw materials may be used, with strong pre-market control  
- **USA:** Wide range of materials allowed, but strict GMP and quality control required  

**Core difference:**  
- EU → Ingredient authorization  
- USA → Process control
